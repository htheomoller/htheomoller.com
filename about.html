Baby Steps

An experiment in group memory

I don't have a computer science degree. I spent twenty years designing books and magazines — setting type, building layouts, thinking in grids and whitespace. I'm a visual thinker who ended up in rural southern Brazil running a wedding venue and raising two daughters.

But I've been watching the same problem repeat across every team I've been part of: the most important knowledge disappears.

It disappears in WhatsApp messages that scroll past. In meetings no one summarizes. In the heads of people who leave. Every team I know has lost something valuable because no one wrote it down — and no one writes it down because the act of documenting feels like work that interrupts the real work.

The researchers call this group memory — or more precisely, transactive memory. How do groups remember? How does a team know what it knows? People have been studying this since the 1980s. The theory is solid. What's new is that the technology to implement it — large language models, semantic search, open tool protocols — now exists.

Murmur is my attempt at solving this. Not a grand vision delivered fully-formed, but baby steps — an experiment by a curious person researching a problem we all share. I started building because I live the problem every day: a small team, fast conversations, decisions made in passing, context that evaporates.

The idea is simple. People talk. The system listens, interprets, and remembers. No forms to fill. No data to enter. Just conversation becoming memory — and memory becoming action.

I don't know if it'll work at scale. I don't know if the theory translates cleanly into product. But I know the problem is real, the research is sound, and the tools are finally here. So I'm building, one step at a time.

Henrique Theo Möller Itapuã, Viamão, Brazil
